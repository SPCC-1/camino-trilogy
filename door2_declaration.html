14.1
Era I: The Tariff Era (founding through Civil War)
The federal government funded itself by taxing trade — import and export duties. This was Hamilton's design. The tariff era aligned federal revenue with the movement of physical goods. It worked for an agrarian, manufacturing economy. It could not survive industrialization.

14.2
Era II: The Income Tax Era (1913–present)
The 16th Amendment: the federal government funds itself by taxing labor income. The income tax era aligned federal revenue with the movement of wages. It worked for a manufacturing, employment-based economy. It cannot survive the automation of labor and the rise of capital income.

15 Contract vs. Covenant — Why the Distinction Matters 4 sub ▶
15.1
What a contract is
Transactional, terminable, governed by self-interest. A contract holds while both parties benefit. When it stops being profitable for either party, it dissolves. Every existing redistributive institution is contractual. The social contract can be rescinded by any political majority.
Subscription required
15.2
What a covenant is
Relational, irrevocable, oriented toward long-term mutual flourishing. A covenant holds regardless of whether it is immediately profitable. It binds across time. It binds even when the parties change. Marriage is a covenant. The Constitution, at its best, is a covenant. The Trust is a covenant.
Subscription required
15.3
Why predistribution requires a covenant
Predistribution requires structural commitments that hold before the capital arrives — before the interests diverge — before the people who made them are still in the room. Only a covenant can hold that long. A contract cannot. This is the philosophical foundation of everything that follows.

15.4
The covenant in American history
The Mayflower Compact. The Constitution. The Emancipation Proclamation. The most durable American institutions are covenantal, not contractual. The Trust operates in this tradition. Not as a religious claim. As a governance architecture.
